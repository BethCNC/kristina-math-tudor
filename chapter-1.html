Section 1.1: Thinking Mathematically

Understanding how mathematical thinking differs from everyday thinking

Key Concepts (Think Like a 5th Grader!)

Deductive Reasoning

What it is: Starting with big rules and figuring out specific answers

Example:

  • • All birds have feathers
  • • A robin is a bird
  • • Therefore: Robins have feathers

Inductive Reasoning

What it is: Looking at patterns and making predictions

Example:

  • • I've seen 10 swans
  • • All 10 were white
  • • Therefore: All swans are white

Study Tips & Tricks

1

Deductive: Think "top-down" - big rules to specific answers

2

Inductive: Think "bottom-up" - patterns to predictions

3

Practice: Look for these types of reasoning in news, ads, and everyday conversations

Practice Question

Question: "Every time I eat ice cream, I get a headache. Therefore, ice cream causes headaches." What type of reasoning is this?

Click to see answer

Answer: This is inductive reasoning because it's making a general conclusion (ice cream causes headaches) based on specific observations (headaches after eating ice cream).

Section 1.2: Estimating and Evaluating

Making smart guesses and checking if answers make sense

Key Concepts (Think Like a 5th Grader!)

Estimation Strategies

Rounding:

Make numbers easier to work with

Example: 47 × 23 ≈ 50 × 20 = 1,000

Benchmark Numbers:

Use familiar numbers (10, 100, 1000)

Example: 87% ≈ 90% (close to 100%)

Evaluating Reasonableness

Ask Yourself:

  • • Does this answer make sense?
  • • Is it too big or too small?
  • • Does it match what I expected?

Red Flag Examples:

  • • A car costs $50 (too cheap!)
  • • A person weighs 2,000 pounds (too heavy!)

Study Tips & Tricks

1

Always estimate first - it helps you catch mistakes!

2

Use order of magnitude - is it 10s, 100s, or 1000s?

3

Trust your gut - if an answer feels wrong, it probably is!

Section 1.3: Problem Solving

Polya's 4-step method for solving any problem

Polya's 4-Step Problem Solving Method

1

Understand the Problem

Ask yourself:

  • • What am I trying to find?
  • • What information do I have?
  • • What are the key words?
  • • Can I draw a picture?
2

Devise a Plan

Choose your strategy:

  • • Draw a diagram
  • • Make a table
  • • Work backwards
  • • Find a pattern
  • • Use a formula
3

Carry Out the Plan

Do the math:

  • • Follow your plan step by step
  • • Show your work clearly
  • • Check each step
  • • Don't rush!
4

Look Back

Check your answer:

  • • Does the answer make sense?
  • • Can I check it another way?
  • • Did I answer the question?
  • • Could I solve it differently?

Worked Example: The Birthday Party Problem

Problem: You're planning a birthday party. You have $50 to spend. Pizza costs $12 each, and drinks cost $2 each. You want to buy 3 pizzas. How many drinks can you buy?

Step 1: Understand

  • • Total money: $50
  • • Pizza: $12 each, need 3
  • • Drinks: $2 each, need to find how many
  • • Find: Number of drinks I can buy

Step 2: Plan

  • • Calculate cost of pizzas
  • • Subtract from total money
  • • Divide remaining money by drink cost

Step 3: Solve

Cost of pizzas: 3 × $12 = $36

Money left: $50 - $36 = $14

Number of drinks: $14 ÷ $2 = 7 drinks

Step 4: Check

  • • 3 pizzas + 7 drinks = $36 + $14 = $50 ✓
  • • Answer makes sense - can buy 7 drinks
  • • Total cost equals budget ✓

Practice Question

Question: A rectangle has a length of 8 inches and an area of 40 square inches. What is the width?

Click to see answer

Step 1: Understand - I need to find the width of a rectangle

Step 2: Plan - Use area formula: Area = length × width

Step 3: Solve - 40 = 8 × width, so width = 40 ÷ 8 = 5 inches

Step 4: Check - 8 × 5 = 40 ✓ Answer: 5 inches
