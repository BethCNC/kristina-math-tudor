11.1 Measures of Center

Mean, median, and mode - finding the center of data

The Three Measures

Mean (Average)

Mean = Σx / n

What: Add all values, divide by count

Best for: Normal distributions

Affected by: Outliers

Median (Middle)

Middle value when ordered

What: Middle of ordered data

Best for: Skewed distributions

Resistant to: Outliers

Mode (Most)

Most frequent value

What: Value appearing most often

Best for: Categorical data

Note: Can have multiple modes

Example: Test Scores [78, 82, 85, 85, 90, 92, 95]

Mean: (78+82+85+85+90+92+95) ÷ 7 = 86.7

Median: Middle value (4th of 7) = 85

Mode: 85 (appears twice)

Practice Problems

Problem 1:

Find the mean and median of the following test scores: 85, 92, 78, 95, 88, 90, 82

For Mean: Add all values: 85 + 92 + 78 + 95 + 88 + 90 + 82 = 610

Divide by count: 610 ÷ 7 = 87.14

For Median: Order data: 78, 82, 85, 88, 90, 92, 95

Middle value: 88 (4th value in ordered list of 7)

Answer: Mean = 87.14, Median = 88

Problem 2:

In a normal distribution with mean = 100 and standard deviation = 15, approximately what percentage of data falls between 85 and 115?

Identify the range: 85 to 115

Distance from mean: 85 = 100 - 15, 115 = 100 + 15

This is mean ± 1 standard deviation

Empirical Rule: About 68% of data falls within 1 standard deviation

Answer: Approximately 68%

11.2 Normal Distribution

Understanding the bell curve and the 68-95-99.7 rule

The Bell Curve

Normal distribution is a symmetric, bell-shaped curve where most data clusters around the center.

• Symmetric around the mean

• Mean = Median = Mode

• Described by mean (μ) and standard deviation (σ)

The 68-95-99.7 Rule (Empirical Rule)

68%

within 1 standard deviation

μ ± 1σ

95%

within 2 standard deviations

μ ± 2σ

99.7%

within 3 standard deviations

μ ± 3σ

11.3 Z-Scores

Standardizing values to compare across different distributions

Z-Score Formula

z = (x - μ) / σ

Population z-score

z = (x - x̄) / s

Sample z-score

Z-score tells you how many standard deviations a value is from the mean

Interpreting Z-Scores

z < 0: Below average

z = 0: Exactly average

z > 0: Above average

|z| > 2: Unusual value

Example: SAT Scores

Given: μ = 500, σ = 100

Your score: 650

Calculate: z = (650-500)/100 = 1.5

Meaning: You scored 1.5 standard deviations above average

Percentile: Better than ~93% of test takers

11.4 Data Types & Visual Displays

Understanding different data types and choosing appropriate graphs

Types of Data

Quantitative: Numbers that can be measured

Heights, weights, test scores, temperatures

Qualitative: Categories or labels

Colors, majors, brands, survey responses

Distribution Shapes

Symmetric: Mean ≈ Median

Right-skewed: Mean > Median

Left-skewed: Mean < Median

Uniform: All values equally likely

Graph Types

Histogram: Show distribution shape

Box Plot: Compare groups, show outliers

Scatter Plot: Relationship between variables

Bar Chart: Compare categories

Pie Chart: Show parts of a whole

Box Plot Components

Box: Middle 50% of data (Q1 to Q3)

Line in box: Median (Q2)

Whiskers: Extend to min/max

Dots: Outliers beyond whiskers

Quick Reference & Formulas

Key Formulas

  • Mean = Σx/n Average of all values
  • z = (x-μ)/σ Standard score
  • 68-95-99.7 Empirical rule

Key Concepts

  • • Mean is affected by outliers, median is not
  • • Normal distribution is symmetric and bell-shaped
  • • Z-scores help compare different distributions
  • • Choose appropriate graphs for data type
