üó≥Ô∏è Voting Methods

1. Plurality Method

The candidate with the most first-place votes wins (even if less than 50%).

Example: In an election with 100 voters:
‚Ä¢ Candidate A: 45 first-place votes
‚Ä¢ Candidate B: 35 first-place votes
‚Ä¢ Candidate C: 20 first-place votes
Winner: Candidate A (plurality, but not majority)

2. Majority Method

A candidate must receive more than 50% of first-place votes to win. If no one has a majority, a runoff election occurs between top two candidates.

Example: 100 total votes, need 51+ to win
‚Ä¢ Candidate A: 45 votes ‚Üí No majority, runoff needed
‚Ä¢ Candidate B: 35 votes
‚Ä¢ Candidate C: 20 votes
Runoff: A vs B

3. Borda Count Method

Voters rank all candidates. Points are assigned based on rank position. Candidate with the most total points wins.

Point System (for n candidates):

  • ‚Ä¢ 1st place: n - 1 points
  • ‚Ä¢ 2nd place: n - 2 points
  • ‚Ä¢ 3rd place: n - 3 points
  • ‚Ä¢ Last place: 0 points
Example: 3 candidates (A, B, C), 10 voters
Rankings: 5 voters rank A‚ÜíB‚ÜíC, 3 voters rank B‚ÜíC‚ÜíA, 2 voters rank C‚ÜíB‚ÜíA
Borda points:
‚Ä¢ A: 5(2pts) + 3(0pts) + 2(0pts) = 10 points
‚Ä¢ B: 5(1pt) + 3(2pts) + 2(1pt) = 13 points
‚Ä¢ C: 5(0pts) + 3(1pt) + 2(2pts) = 7 points
Winner: Candidate B

4. Instant Runoff Voting (IRV)

Voters rank candidates by preference. If no one has a majority of first-place votes, eliminate the candidate with fewest first-place votes and redistribute those ballots to next preferences. Repeat until someone has a majority.

Example: 100 voters, 3 candidates
Round 1: A: 40, B: 35, C: 25 ‚Üí Eliminate C
Round 2: Redistribute C's votes by 2nd preferences
Final: A: 48, B: 52 ‚Üí Winner: B

5. Pairwise Comparison (Condorcet Method)

Compare each candidate head-to-head with every other candidate. The candidate who wins the most one-on-one matchups wins overall.

Example: 3 candidates (A, B, C)
‚Ä¢ A vs B: A wins (60-40)
‚Ä¢ A vs C: A wins (55-45)
‚Ä¢ B vs C: B wins (65-35)
Winner: Candidate A (2 wins)

üìä Apportionment Methods

Apportionment determines how to fairly divide seats (e.g., in Congress) among states based on population.

Standard Divisor (SD)

SD = Total Population / Total Seats

The "ideal" number of people per representative. Used as a starting point for all apportionment methods.

Example: A state has 12,000,000 people and 50 seats in the legislature.
SD = 12,000,000 / 50 = 240,000 people per seat
This means ideally, each representative should represent 240,000 people.

Standard Quota (SQ)

SQ = Subgroup Population / Standard Divisor

The "fair share" of seats for each state or district based on its population. Usually not a whole number, so rounding methods are needed.

Example: District A has 720,000 people; SD = 240,000
SQ = 720,000 / 240,000 = 3.00 seats

Example 2: District B has 550,000 people; SD = 240,000
SQ = 550,000 / 240,000 = 2.29 seats
District B deserves about 2.29 seats, but we must round to give whole seats.

Hamilton Method

Steps:

  1. Calculate Standard Divisor (SD) and each state's Standard Quota (SQ)
  2. Give each state the integer part of its quota (round down initially)
  3. If seats remain, give them one-by-one to states with the largest fractional remainders
Example: 100 seats, 3 states
‚Ä¢ State A: SQ = 45.6 ‚Üí Initial: 45, Remainder: 0.6
‚Ä¢ State B: SQ = 32.9 ‚Üí Initial: 32, Remainder: 0.9
‚Ä¢ State C: SQ = 21.5 ‚Üí Initial: 21, Remainder: 0.5
Total initial: 45 + 32 + 21 = 98 (2 seats left)
Extra seats go to: B (0.9) and A (0.6)
Final: A: 46, B: 33, C: 21

Jefferson Method

Use a modified divisor (smaller than SD) and round all quotas down. Adjust divisor until the correct total number of seats is reached.

Advantage: Favors larger states
Method: Trial and error to find the right modified divisor

Webster Method

Use a modified divisor and round quotas to the nearest whole number (standard rounding: 0.5 rounds up). Adjust divisor until correct total is reached.

Advantage: Generally considered the fairest method
Currently used: For U.S. House of Representatives

‚ö†Ô∏è Voting Paradoxes & Fairness Criteria

Arrow's Impossibility Theorem

No voting system can satisfy all fairness criteria simultaneously (majority, monotonicity, independence of irrelevant alternatives, etc.).

Alabama Paradox

In Hamilton's method, increasing the total number of seats can cause a state to lose a seat!

Quota Rule

A state's apportionment should be either the floor or ceiling of its standard quota. Hamilton satisfies this; Jefferson and Webster do not always.

üí° Real-World Applications

U.S. House of Representatives

435 seats apportioned among 50 states using the Webster method based on census data every 10 years.

City Council Elections

Many cities use ranked-choice voting (IRV) to elect mayors and council members.

Awards & Rankings

Borda count is used in sports rankings (e.g., Heisman Trophy) and academic awards.

International Bodies

UN, EU Parliament, and other international organizations use apportionment to distribute seats fairly among member nations.
