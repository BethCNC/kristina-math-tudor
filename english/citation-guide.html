In-Text Citations

What are In-Text Citations?

In-text citations (also called parenthetical citations) are brief references in your paper that tell readers where information came from. They correspond to fuller entries on your Works Cited page.

Basic Format: (Author Last Name Page Number)

Basic In-Text Citation Examples

1. Author + Page Number

(Smith 42).

Note: No comma between author and page number

2. Author Named in Sentence

According to Smith, "happiness is subjective" (42).

3. No Page Number (Web Sources)

(Smith).

4. Multiple Authors (2)

(Smith and Jones 45).

5. Multiple Authors (3+)

(Smith et al. 23).

6. No Author (Use Title)

("Impact of Social Media" 12).

Use quotes for article titles, italics for book titles

Where Does the Citation Go?

End of Sentence (Most Common)

Social media affects mental health (Smith 15).

Citation comes BEFORE the period

After a Quotation

Smith argues that "social media harms teens" (42).

Citation after quote, before period

Block Quote (4+ lines)

Smith explains: Social media companies design their algorithms to maximize engagement, which often means showing users content that triggers strong emotions. (45)

Citation comes AFTER the period in block quotes

Common Mistakes

❌ Wrong: (Smith, 42)
✅ Right: (Smith 42)
No comma between author and page
❌ Wrong: (page 42)
✅ Right: (Smith 42)
Don't write "page" or "pg."
❌ Wrong: Smith says this (42).
✅ Right: Smith says "this" (42).
Use quotation marks for exact words

Quick Tips

  • ✅ Cite EVERY time you use someone else's ideas (quote or paraphrase)
  • ✅ Match your in-text citation to your Works Cited entry
  • ✅ Place citation as close as possible to the borrowed material
  • ✅ When in doubt, cite it—better safe than plagiarizing
