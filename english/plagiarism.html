Understanding Plagiarism

Academic Integrity is Required

Plagiarism results in serious consequences, including failing the assignment, failing the course, or academic probation.

What is Plagiarism?

Plagiarism is using someone else's words, ideas, or work without giving proper credit. It's essentially stealing intellectual property.

Even if you didn't intend to plagiarize, it's still plagiarism.

Not knowing the rules doesn't excuse the violation. It's your responsibility to learn how to cite sources correctly.

Types of Plagiarism

1. Direct Plagiarism (Copy-Paste)

Copying text word-for-word without quotation marks and citation.

Example of plagiarism:
Social media has negative effects on teenage mental health.

Correct version:
According to Smith, "social media has negative effects on teenage mental health" (42).

2. Paraphrasing Without Citation

Rewording someone else's ideas without giving credit.

Original: "Instagram's algorithm prioritizes appearance-focused content."

Plagiarized paraphrase: Instagram's system gives priority to posts about appearance.

Correct paraphrase: Smith argues that Instagram's system gives priority to posts about appearance (15).

3. Patchwork Plagiarism

Mixing copied phrases from sources without proper citation.

4. Self-Plagiarism

Reusing your own previous work without permission.

5. AI-Generated Content Without Disclosure

Using ChatGPT or other AI tools without instructor permission or proper attribution.

How to Avoid Plagiarism

✅ Always cite your sources

Use in-text citations for EVERY idea that comes from someone else, even if you paraphrase.

✅ Use quotation marks for exact words

If you copy even a short phrase, put it in quotation marks and cite it.

✅ Paraphrase properly

Put the idea in your own words AND cite the source. Changing a few words isn't enough!

✅ Take careful notes

Track citation info as you research so you don't lose track of sources.

✅ When in doubt, cite it

It's better to over-cite than to risk plagiarism.

What Needs a Citation?

❌ Must be Cited:

  • • Direct quotations
  • • Paraphrased ideas
  • • Statistics and data
  • • Specific facts or figures
  • • Someone else's theories or arguments
  • • Images, graphs, charts from sources

✅ Does NOT Need Citation:

  • • Your own ideas and experiences
  • • Common knowledge (widely known facts)
  • • Historical dates everyone knows
  • • Well-known quotations ("To be or not to be")

Consequences of Plagiarism

1st Offense: Failing grade on assignment, possible course failure
2nd Offense: Course failure, academic probation
Repeated Offenses: Suspension or expulsion from CPCC
