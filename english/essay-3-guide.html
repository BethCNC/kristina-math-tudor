Essay 3

Analytical Essay

Due Date: Friday, November 15, 2025 at 11:59 PM

Submit via Brightspace (D2L). Late submissions subject to grade penalties.

Assignment Overview

For this assignment, you will write an analytical essay that examines a text, concept, or issue in depth. You'll develop a clear thesis and support it with evidence from credible sources.

Length

1000-1250 words

Format

MLA 9th Edition

Sources

Minimum 2 credible sources

Learning Objectives

  • Develop a clear, analytical thesis statement
  • Support claims with textual evidence and analysis
  • Integrate sources using proper MLA citations
  • Organize ideas logically with clear transitions
  • Demonstrate critical thinking and interpretation

Requirements

Content Requirements

  • • Analytical thesis that makes a clear claim about your topic
  • • Evidence from sources integrated smoothly into your writing
  • • Analysis and interpretation that goes beyond summary
  • • Critical thinking demonstrated throughout
  • • Coherent organization with clear introduction, body, and conclusion

Technical Requirements

  • • 1000-1250 words (approximately 4-5 pages, double-spaced)
  • • MLA 9th Edition format (heading, header, margins, spacing)
  • • Minimum 2 credible sources (academic journals, books, reputable websites)
  • • Proper in-text citations for all quoted and paraphrased material
  • • Works Cited page in MLA format (does not count toward word count)
  • • 12-point font (Times New Roman or similar)
  • • Double-spaced throughout
  • • 1-inch margins on all sides

Writing Process

1. Choose Your Topic & Develop Your Thesis

Select a text, concept, or issue you can analyze in depth. Develop a specific, arguable thesis that presents your interpretation.

Example thesis: "While Adam Grant argues that pursuing happiness directly often backfires, his analysis overlooks the role of cultural context in shaping how people define and seek happiness."

2. Research & Gather Evidence

Find at least 2 credible sources that support your analysis. Take notes and track citation information as you research.

  • • Use CPCC library databases for academic sources
  • • Evaluate source credibility carefully
  • • Record all citation details immediately

3. Create an Outline

Organize your ideas before drafting. Each body paragraph should have a clear point that supports your thesis.

Basic structure:
I. Introduction with thesis
II. Body paragraph 1 (first analytical point)
III. Body paragraph 2 (second analytical point)
IV. Body paragraph 3 (counterargument/complexity)
V. Conclusion

4. Write Your Draft

Write your first complete draft. Focus on getting your ideas down; you'll revise later.

  • • Start with body paragraphs (easier than introduction)
  • • Include in-text citations as you write
  • • Don't worry about perfection yet

5. Revise & Edit

Improve your draft through multiple rounds of revision. Check organization, evidence, and mechanics.

  • • Read aloud to catch awkward phrasing
  • • Verify all citations are properly formatted
  • • Use the revision checklist below

Pre-Submission Checklist

Common Mistakes to Avoid

❌ Summarizing instead of analyzing

Don't just retell what the text says. Explain what it means, how it works, and why it matters.

❌ Weak or missing thesis

Your thesis should be specific and arguable, not a statement of fact.

❌ Missing citations

Cite ANY idea that's not your own, even if you paraphrase.

❌ Using unreliable sources

Wikipedia, random blogs, and opinion sites are not credible academic sources.
