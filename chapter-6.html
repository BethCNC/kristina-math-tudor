Tutorial 1: Simple Interest

What is Simple Interest?

Simple interest is calculated only on the principal (original amount). It doesn't compound - you earn the same amount each period.

Simple Interest Formula

I = P × r × t

I = Interest earned

P = Principal (starting amount)

r = Annual interest rate (as decimal)

t = Time in years

Step-by-Step Example

Problem: You invest $1,000 at 5% simple interest for 3 years. How much interest will you earn?

1

Identify the values:

  • P = $1,000 (principal)
  • r = 5% = 0.05 (rate as decimal)
  • t = 3 years
2

Apply the formula:

I = P × r × t

I = $1,000 × 0.05 × 3

3

Calculate:

I = $150

Total amount = Principal + Interest = $1,000 + $150 = $1,150

Practice Problem

Try this: Sarah borrows $2,500 at 4% simple interest for 2.5 years. How much interest will she pay?

Show Solution

P = $2,500, r = 0.04, t = 2.5

I = $2,500 × 0.04 × 2.5 = $250

Sarah will pay $250 in interest.

Tutorial 2: Compound Interest

What is Compound Interest?

Compound interest means you earn interest on your interest! Your money grows faster because each period you earn interest on a larger amount.

Compound Interest Formula

A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt)

A = Final amount

P = Principal (starting amount)

r = Annual interest rate (as decimal)

n = Number of times compounded per year

t = Time in years

Common Compounding Frequencies

• Annual: n = 1
• Quarterly: n = 4
• Monthly: n = 12
• Daily: n = 365

Step-by-Step Example

Problem: You invest $1,000 at 5% compounded quarterly for 3 years. What's the final amount?

1

Identify the values:

  • P = $1,000
  • r = 0.05
  • n = 4 (quarterly)
  • t = 3 years
2

Set up the formula:

A = $1,000(1 + 0.05/4)^(4×3)

A = $1,000(1 + 0.0125)^12

3

Calculate:

A = $1,000(1.0125)^12

A = $1,000(1.1608)

A = $1,160.80

Interest earned = $1,160.80 - $1,000 = $160.80

Simple vs Compound Interest Comparison

Same example: $1,000 at 5% for 3 years

Simple Interest:

Interest = $150

Total = $1,150

Compound Interest:

Interest = $160.80

Total = $1,160.80

Compound interest earned $10.80 more!

Tutorial 3: Understanding Loans

Key Loan Concepts

  • Longer loan terms = lower monthly payments BUT more total interest
  • Extra payments toward principal reduce total interest significantly
  • Always compare total cost, not just monthly payments

Loan Comparison Example

Scenario: $20,000 car loan at 6% annual interest

Option A: 4-Year Loan
  • Monthly payment: ~$469
  • Total payments: $22,512
  • Total interest: $2,512
Option B: 6-Year Loan
  • Monthly payment: ~$331
  • Total payments: $23,832
  • Total interest: $3,832

💡 The 6-year loan costs $1,320 more in total interest!

Smart Loan Tips

  • Put extra payments toward the principal, not future payments
  • Even $25-50 extra per month can save hundreds in interest
  • Shop around for the best interest rates
  • Consider the total cost, not just the monthly payment

Tutorial 4: Progressive Tax Brackets

How Progressive Taxes Work

You don't pay the same rate on all your income! Higher rates only apply to income in higher brackets.

Sample Tax Brackets (Simplified)

$0 - $10,000 10%
$10,001 - $40,000 12%
$40,001 - $85,000 22%

Tax Calculation Example

Problem: Calculate taxes on $50,000 income using the brackets above.

1

First bracket: $0-$10,000 at 10%

$10,000 × 0.10 = $1,000

2

Second bracket: $10,001-$40,000 at 12%

$30,000 × 0.12 = $3,600

(This is $40,000 - $10,000 = $30,000 in this bracket)

3

Third bracket: $40,001-$50,000 at 22%

$10,000 × 0.22 = $2,200

(This is $50,000 - $40,000 = $10,000 in this bracket)

4

Total tax:

$1,000 + $3,600 + $2,200 = $6,800

Effective tax rate: $6,800 ÷ $50,000 = 13.6%

Key Tax Terms

  • Marginal Tax Rate: The rate on your last dollar earned (22% in the example)
  • Effective Tax Rate: Your total tax ÷ total income (13.6% in the example)
  • Tax Bracket: You're "in" the highest bracket that applies to your income

Tutorial 5: Creating a Budget

The 50/30/20 Rule

50% Needs

Housing, food, utilities, minimum debt payments

30% Wants

Entertainment, dining out, hobbies

20% Savings

Emergency fund, retirement, extra debt payments

Budget Example: $4,000 Monthly Income

Needs (50% = $2,000)
  • Rent: $1,200
  • Groceries: $400
  • Utilities: $150
  • Car payment: $250
Wants (30% = $1,200)
  • Dining out: $300
  • Entertainment: $200
  • Gym: $50
  • Shopping: $400
  • Miscellaneous: $250
Savings (20% = $800)
  • Emergency fund: $400
  • Retirement: $300
  • Extra debt payment: $100

Budgeting Tips for Success

  • Track everything for one month before making a budget - you need to know where your money actually goes
  • Start with housing <30% of income - this gives you flexibility for other goals
  • Build an emergency fund first - aim for 3-6 months of expenses
  • Use percentages, not fixed amounts - this makes your budget scalable as income changes
  • Review and adjust monthly - budgets are living documents, not set in stone

Personal Finance Formula Sheet

Simple Interest

I = P × r × t

I = Interest earned

P = Principal (starting amount)

r = Annual interest rate (decimal)

t = Time in years

Compound Interest

A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt)

A = Final amount

P = Principal

r = Annual interest rate (decimal)

n = Compounding frequency per year

t = Time in years

Effective Tax Rate

Effective Rate = Total Tax ÷ Total Income

Shows your actual tax burden as a percentage

Always lower than marginal rate

Budget Percentages (50/30/20 Rule)

50% Needs: Housing, food, utilities, minimum debt
30% Wants: Entertainment, dining out, hobbies
20% Savings: Emergency fund, retirement, extra payments

Key Interest Conversions

Percentage to Decimal: 5% = 0.05

Annual to Monthly Rate: Annual ÷ 12

Years to Months: Years × 12

Remember: Time units must match rate period!

Common Compounding Frequencies

Annual: n = 1
Semi-annual: n = 2
Quarterly: n = 4
Monthly: n = 12
Weekly: n = 52
Daily: n = 365

Annual Percentage Yield (APY)

APY = ((1 + r/n)^n - 1) × 100%

Shows the actual annual return including compounding effects

Higher compounding frequency = higher APY

Present Value

PV = A / (1 + r/n)^(nt)

How much you need to invest today to reach a future goal

PV = Present Value, A = Future Amount desired

Annuity Formulas

Future Value of Annuity:

FV = PMT × [((1 + r/n)^(nt) - 1) / (r/n)]

Payment Amount for Target Future Value:

PMT = FV × [r/n / ((1 + r/n)^(nt) - 1)]

For regular savings or retirement planning

Loan Payment Formula

PMT = (P × r/n) / (1 - (1 + r/n)^(-nt))

Calculate monthly payments for loans

P = Loan amount, PMT = Monthly payment

Maximum Purchase Price

Max Price = PMT × [(1 - (1 + r/n)^(-nt)) / (r/n)]

How much you can afford with a given monthly payment

Useful for car loans, mortgages

ADHD-Friendly Study Tips for Personal Finance

Memory Tricks & Patterns

Simple vs Compound: Simple = "SAME every time" (same interest amount each period)

Compound: "COMPOUND = GROWS" (interest grows because it's calculated on more money each time)

Tax Brackets Memory: Think "climbing stairs" - you pay the rate for each "step" (bracket) you reach, not the same rate for the whole climb

50/30/20 Rule: "HALF for must-haves, THIRD for fun stuff, ONE-FIFTH for future me"

Breaking Down Complex Problems

Interest Problems:

  1. Circle/highlight the given numbers
  2. Convert percentage to decimal
  3. Match time units (years with annual rate)
  4. Plug into formula carefully

Tax Calculations:

  1. Draw the tax brackets as steps
  2. Calculate tax for each bracket separately
  3. Add up all the bracket taxes
  4. Check: effective rate should be less than top bracket

Time Management & Focus

Study Sessions:

  • 15-minute focused bursts with 5-minute breaks
  • Do ONE type of problem at a time (all simple interest, then all compound)
  • Use timer to stay on track

Reduce Overwhelm:

  • Cover up other problems while working on one
  • Use different colored pens for different steps
  • Say the steps out loud as you work

Avoid Common Mistakes

Interest Mistakes:

  • Forgetting to convert % to decimal (5% = 0.05)
  • Using wrong time units (months vs years)
  • Mixing up simple and compound formulas

Tax Mistakes:

  • Applying highest rate to all income
  • Forgetting to subtract bracket minimums
  • Confusing marginal vs effective rates
