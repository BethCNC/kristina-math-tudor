6.1 Simple Interest

Calculate interest that doesn't compound

Simple Interest Formula

I = P × r × t

I = Interest earned

P = Principal (starting amount)

r = Annual interest rate (as decimal)

t = Time in years

Step-by-Step Process
1 Identify P, r, and t
2 Convert percentage to decimal
3 Multiply P × r × t
4 Total = P + I
Example

Problem: $1,000 at 5% for 3 years

P: $1,000

r: 5% = 0.05

t: 3 years

I: 1,000 × 0.05 × 3 = $150

Total: $1,000 + $150 = $1,150

Practice Problems

Problem 1:

You deposit $2,500 in a savings account that earns 3% simple interest per year. How much interest will you earn after 4 years?

Formula: I = P × r × t

Given: P = $2,500, r = 0.03, t = 4 years

Calculate: I = $2,500 × 0.03 × 4 = $300

Answer: $300 in interest

Problem 2:

Your monthly budget is $3,200. You spend $960 on rent, $400 on food, $200 on transportation, and $300 on utilities. What percentage of your budget is left for other expenses?

Total expenses: $960 + $400 + $200 + $300 = $1,860

Amount left: $3,200 - $1,860 = $1,340

Percentage: ($1,340 ÷ $3,200) × 100% = 41.875%

Answer: 41.9% of budget remaining

6.2 Compound Interest

Calculate interest that grows on interest

Compound Interest Formula

A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt)

A = Final amount

P = Principal

r = Annual rate (decimal)

n = Compounding frequency per year

t = Time in years

Common Frequencies
Annual: n = 1
Quarterly: n = 4
Monthly: n = 12
Daily: n = 365
Example

Problem: $1,000 at 5% quarterly for 3 years

Setup: A = 1000(1 + 0.05/4)^(4×3)

Simplify: A = 1000(1.0125)^12

Calculate: A = 1000(1.1608)

Result: A = $1,160.80

6.3 Progressive Tax Brackets

Understanding how tax brackets work

How Progressive Taxes Work

You pay different rates on different portions of your income

Think of climbing stairs - you pay the rate for each "step" you reach

Sample Tax Brackets
$0 - $10,000 10%
$10,001 - $40,000 12%
$40,001 - $85,000 22%
Example: $50,000 Income

Bracket 1: $10,000 × 0.10 = $1,000

Bracket 2: $30,000 × 0.12 = $3,600

Bracket 3: $10,000 × 0.22 = $2,200

Total Tax: $6,800

Effective Rate: 13.6%

6.4 Budget Planning

The 50/30/20 budgeting rule

The 50/30/20 Rule

50% Needs
  • • Housing
  • • Food
  • • Utilities
  • • Transportation
  • • Minimum debt payments
30% Wants
  • • Entertainment
  • • Dining out
  • • Hobbies
  • • Shopping
  • • Subscriptions
20% Savings
  • • Emergency fund
  • • Retirement
  • • Extra debt payments
  • • Future goals
  • • Investments
Example: $4,000 Monthly Income

Needs: $2,000 (50%)

Wants: $1,200 (30%)

Savings: $800 (20%)

Quick Reference & Formulas

Key Formulas

  • I = P × r × t Simple interest
  • A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt) Compound interest
  • Rate = Total Tax ÷ Income Effective tax rate

Key Concepts

  • • Simple interest stays the same each period
  • • Compound interest grows over time
  • • Tax brackets apply to income ranges
  • • Budgets help manage spending and saving
