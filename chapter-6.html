6.1

Understanding Interest

Learn how simple interest works and applies to financial situations

Key Concepts (Think Like a 5th Grader!)

Simple Interest

What it is: Interest calculated only on the original amount (like a flat fee!)

Formula:

I = Prt

Where:

  • • I = Interest earned
  • • P = Principal (starting amount)
  • • r = annual interest rate (as decimal)
  • • t = time in years

Principal, Rate, Time

What it is: The three key ingredients for interest!

Relationship:

• Rate must be in decimal form (5% = 0.05)

• Time must match the rate period

• Annual rate needs time in years

Simple vs Compound

What it is: Two different ways interest can work!

Simple Interest:

• Calculated only on principal

Compound Interest:

• Interest on interest (grows faster!)

Time Conversions

What it is: Converting time periods to years!

Common Conversions:

  • • 6 months = 0.5 years
  • • 9 months = 0.75 years
  • • 18 months = 1.5 years
  • • 30 months = 2.5 years

Study Tips & Tricks

1

Simple interest is calculated only on principal

2

Rate must be in decimal form (5% = 0.05)

3

Time must match the rate period (annual rate needs time in years)

Simple Interest Formula

I = Prt

  • I = Interest earned (dollars)
  • P = Principal (initial amount)
  • r = Annual interest rate (as decimal)
  • t = Time (in years)
Key Points
  • • Interest stays constant each period
  • • Convert percentage to decimal (5% = 0.05)
  • • Total amount = Principal + Interest
  • • Time must be in years
Step-by-Step Process
  1. Identify P, r, and t from the problem
  2. Convert percentage to decimal (÷100)
  3. Convert time to years if needed
  4. Multiply: Interest = P × r × t
  5. Find total: Total = Principal + Interest
Common Time Conversions

• 6 months = 0.5 years

• 9 months = 0.75 years

• 18 months = 1.5 years

• 30 months = 2.5 years

Worked Examples

Example 1: Basic Simple Interest

You invest $2,500 in a savings account that pays 4% simple interest per year. How much interest will you earn after 3 years?

Step 1: Identify the values

  • • P = $2,500
  • • r = 4% = 0.04
  • • t = 3 years

Step 2: Apply the formula

I = Prt = 2,500 × 0.04 × 3

Step 3: Calculate

I = 2,500 × 0.04 × 3 = $300

Answer: You'll earn $300 in interest

Total amount: $2,500 + $300 = $2,800

Example 2: Finding the Principal

What principal amount would earn $180 in simple interest at 6% annual rate for 2 years?

Step 1: Identify what we know

  • • I = $180
  • • r = 6% = 0.06
  • • t = 2 years
  • • P = ? (what we're finding)

Step 2: Rearrange the formula

I = Prt → P = I ÷ (rt)

Step 3: Calculate

P = 180 ÷ (0.06 × 2) = 180 ÷ 0.12 = $1,500

Answer: The principal is $1,500

Practice Problems

Problem 1:

A loan of $5,000 charges 7% simple interest per year. How much total will be owed after 18 months?

Given: P = $5,000, r = 0.07, t = 18 months = 1.5 years

Interest: I = 5,000 × 0.07 × 1.5 = $525

Total owed: $5,000 + $525 = $5,525

Problem 2:

How long will it take for $800 to earn $96 in simple interest at 4% annually?

Given: P = $800, I = $96, r = 0.04, t = ?

Rearrange: t = I ÷ (Pr) = 96 ÷ (800 × 0.04) = 96 ÷ 32 = 3 years

6.2

Saving and Investing

Understanding compound interest and how money grows over time

Compound Interest Formula

A = P(1 + r/n)^(nt)

  • A = Final amount
  • P = Principal (initial deposit)
  • r = Annual interest rate (decimal)
  • n = Times compounded per year
  • t = Number of years
Compounding Frequencies
  • • Annually: n = 1
  • • Quarterly: n = 4
  • • Monthly: n = 12
  • • Daily: n = 365
Why Compound Interest is Powerful

With compound interest, you earn interest on your interest! This creates exponential growth over time. Albert Einstein reportedly called compound interest "the eighth wonder of the world."

Worked Examples

Example 1: Monthly Compounding

You deposit $1,000 in an account that pays 6% annual interest, compounded monthly. What will the account be worth after 5 years?

Step 1: Identify the values

  • • P = $1,000
  • • r = 6% = 0.06
  • • n = 12 (monthly)
  • • t = 5 years

Step 2: Set up the formula

A = 1,000(1 + 0.06/12)^(12×5)

Step 3: Simplify inside parentheses

A = 1,000(1 + 0.005)^60 = 1,000(1.005)^60

Step 4: Calculate

A = 1,000(1.3489) = $1,348.90

Answer: The account will be worth $1,348.90

Interest earned: $1,348.90 - $1,000 = $348.90

Example 2: Comparing Compounding Frequencies

Compare $5,000 invested at 8% for 10 years with annual vs. quarterly compounding.

Annual Compounding (n = 1):

A = 5,000(1 + 0.08/1)^(1×10) = 5,000(1.08)^10

A = 5,000(2.159) = $10,794.62

Quarterly Compounding (n = 4):

A = 5,000(1 + 0.08/4)^(4×10) = 5,000(1.02)^40

A = 5,000(2.208) = $11,040.20

Difference: $11,040.20 - $10,794.62 = $245.58

More frequent compounding earns more money!

Example 3: Annual Percentage Yield (APY)

An account advertises 5% annual interest compounded daily. What is the actual annual percentage yield?

APY Formula: APY = (1 + r/n)^n - 1

Step 1: Substitute values

APY = (1 + 0.05/365)^365 - 1

Step 2: Calculate

APY = (1.0001370)^365 - 1

APY = 1.0513 - 1 = 0.0513

Answer: APY = 5.13%

The effective yield is slightly higher than the stated rate due to compounding.

Practice Problems

Problem 1:

$2,500 is invested at 7% annual interest, compounded quarterly. Find the value after 8 years.

Given: P = $2,500, r = 0.07, n = 4, t = 8

Formula: A = 2,500(1 + 0.07/4)^(4×8) = 2,500(1.0175)^32

Calculate: A = 2,500(1.749) = $4,372.50

Problem 2:

What principal amount, invested at 4% compounded annually, would grow to $15,000 in 12 years?

Rearrange: P = A ÷ (1 + r/n)^(nt)

Calculate: P = 15,000 ÷ (1.04)^12 = 15,000 ÷ 1.601 = $9,370.74

6.3

Borrowing Money

Understanding loans, monthly payments, and total interest costs

Loan Payment Formula

PMT = P × [r(1+r)^n] / [(1+r)^n - 1]

  • PMT = Monthly payment
  • P = Loan amount (principal)
  • r = Monthly interest rate (annual rate ÷ 12)
  • n = Total number of payments
Key Facts About Loans
  • • Early payments are mostly interest
  • • Later payments are mostly principal
  • • Total interest = (PMT × n) - P
  • • Longer term = higher total interest
Loan Payment Process
  1. Convert annual rate to monthly (÷12)
  2. Calculate total payments (years × 12)
  3. Apply the payment formula
  4. Find total paid and total interest
Loan Comparison Tips
  • • Lower rate = less total interest
  • • Shorter term = higher payment, less interest
  • • Extra payments save thousands in interest
  • • Shop around for best rates

Worked Example

Car Loan Calculation

You need to borrow $24,000 for a car at 6.5% annual interest for 4 years. Calculate the monthly payment and total interest.

Step 1: Identify and convert values

  • • P = $24,000
  • • Annual rate = 6.5%, so monthly r = 0.065 ÷ 12 = 0.0054167
  • • n = 4 years × 12 = 48 payments

Step 2: Apply the formula

PMT = 24,000 × [0.0054167(1.0054167)^48] / [(1.0054167)^48 - 1]

Step 3: Calculate components

(1.0054167)^48 = 1.2979

PMT = 24,000 × [0.0054167 × 1.2979] / [1.2979 - 1]

PMT = 24,000 × 0.007029 / 0.2979 = $566.88

Monthly Payment: $566.88

Total Paid: $566.88 × 48 = $27,210.24

Total Interest: $27,210.24 - $24,000 = $3,210.24

6.4

Federal Revenue

Understanding progressive tax brackets and effective tax rates

Progressive Tax System

How Tax Brackets Work

You pay different tax rates on different portions of your income. Think of it like climbing stairs - you pay the rate for each "step" you reach, not the highest rate on all your income.

2024 Tax Brackets (Simplified Example)
$0 - $11,000 10%
$11,001 - $44,725 12%
$44,726 - $95,375 22%
$95,376+ 24%
Key Terms
  • Marginal Tax Rate: Rate on your last dollar earned
  • Effective Tax Rate: Average rate on all income
  • Tax Bracket: Income range with specific rate
  • Progressive: Higher earners pay higher rates
Calculation Steps
  1. Start with the lowest bracket
  2. Calculate tax for each bracket you fall into
  3. Add up all bracket taxes
  4. Find effective rate: Total tax ÷ Income

Worked Example

Tax Calculation for $60,000 Income

Calculate the federal income tax and effective tax rate for someone earning $60,000.

Bracket 1: $11,000 at 10%

Tax = $11,000 × 0.10 = $1,100

Bracket 2: $33,725 at 12% ($44,725 - $11,000)

Tax = $33,725 × 0.12 = $4,047

Bracket 3: $15,275 at 22% ($60,000 - $44,725)

Tax = $15,275 × 0.22 = $3,360.50

Total Tax: $1,100 + $4,047 + $3,360.50 = $8,507.50

Effective Rate: $8,507.50 ÷ $60,000 = 14.18%

Marginal Rate: 22% (highest bracket reached)

Practice Problem

Tax Calculation:

Calculate the total federal tax and effective tax rate for someone earning $85,000 annually.

Bracket 1: $11,000 × 0.10 = $1,100

Bracket 2: $33,725 × 0.12 = $4,047

Bracket 3: $40,275 × 0.22 = $8,860.50 ($85,000 - $44,725)

Total Tax: $14,007.50

Effective Rate: 16.48%

Budgeting

Creating effective budgets using the 50/30/20 rule

The 50/30/20 Budget Rule

50%
NEEDS
  • • Housing (rent/mortgage)
  • • Utilities
  • • Groceries
  • • Transportation
  • • Insurance
  • • Minimum debt payments
30%
WANTS
  • • Entertainment
  • • Dining out
  • • Hobbies
  • • Shopping
  • • Subscriptions
  • • Vacations
20%
SAVINGS
  • • Emergency fund
  • • Retirement contributions
  • • Extra debt payments
  • • Future goals
  • • Investments
Budget Tips for Success
  • • Track spending for a month before creating a budget
  • • Use percentages as guidelines, not rigid rules
  • • Prioritize building an emergency fund first
  • • Automate savings to make it effortless
  • • Review and adjust your budget regularly
